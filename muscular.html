Overview

The muscular system consists of over 600 muscles that facilitate movement, maintain posture, and regulate essential functions like blood circulation.

Muscles work in coordination with the skeletal and nervous systems to produce voluntary and involuntary movements. They also play a role in maintaining body temperature through heat generation during contraction.

Types of Muscle Tissue

  • Skeletal Muscle: Voluntary muscles attached to bones, enabling movement. These muscles are striated and can be consciously controlled.
  • Cardiac Muscle: Involuntary muscle found only in the heart, responsible for pumping blood. Cardiac muscle cells are branched and interconnected for synchronized contractions.
  • Smooth Muscle: Involuntary muscles lining the organs, controlling functions like digestion, blood vessel constriction, and respiration. These muscles are non-striated and work automatically.

Each muscle type has unique structural and functional properties suited to its role in the body.

Muscle Contraction & Physiology

Muscles contract via the sliding filament theory, where actin and myosin proteins interact. During contraction, myosin heads bind to actin filaments and pull them inward, shortening the muscle fiber.

  • ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate): Powers muscle contractions by providing energy for myosin head movement and detachment.
  • Calcium Ions: Released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, they enable actin-myosin binding by exposing binding sites on actin.
  • Neuromuscular Junction: The synapse where a motor neuron communicates with a muscle fiber, triggering contraction via neurotransmitters like acetylcholine.
  • Sarcomere: The basic contractile unit of muscle fiber, composed of repeating actin and myosin filaments.
  • Muscle Fatigue: Occurs when muscles lose the ability to contract efficiently, often due to depletion of ATP or accumulation of lactic acid.

Muscle contraction is a highly regulated process involving electrical signals, chemical messengers, and structural proteins working together to produce movement.

Muscle Groups & Functions

  • Flexors: Decrease joint angles (e.g., biceps brachii bends the elbow).
  • Extensors: Increase joint angles (e.g., triceps brachii straightens the elbow).
  • Abductors: Move limbs away from the midline (e.g., deltoid raises the arm sideways).
  • Adductors: Move limbs toward the midline (e.g., adductor longus brings the thigh inward).
  • Rotators: Rotate a bone around its axis (e.g., rotator cuff muscles in the shoulder).
  • Levators: Lift a body part (e.g., levator scapulae elevates the scapula).
  • Depressors: Lower a body part (e.g., depressor anguli oris lowers the corners of the mouth).

Major muscle groups include the pectorals (chest), latissimus dorsi (back), quadriceps (front thigh), hamstrings (back thigh), gastrocnemius (calf), and gluteals (buttocks). Each group works together to enable complex movements and maintain stability.

Muscle Disorders

  • Muscular Dystrophy: Genetic condition leading to progressive muscle weakening and loss of muscle mass. Different types affect various muscle groups and can impact mobility and heart function.
  • Myasthenia Gravis: Autoimmune disorder causing muscle fatigue and weakness, especially after periods of activity. It often affects muscles controlling the eyes, face, and swallowing.
  • Muscle Strains: Damage from overstretching or tearing muscle fibers, commonly caused by excessive physical activity or improper lifting techniques. Symptoms include pain, swelling, and limited movement.
  • Cramping: Sudden, involuntary muscle contractions that can be painful. Causes include dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, or prolonged exercise.

If you experience persistent muscle weakness, pain, or cramps, consult a healthcare professional for diagnosis and treatment.
